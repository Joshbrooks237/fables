# EVERYTHING MUST GO

### a novel

**Joshua Brooks**

---

*for anyone who has ever stood in a garage*
*holding something they cannot throw away*
*and cannot keep*

---

## 1. The Notice

The notice arrived on a Tuesday, which is the most insulting day of the week for bad news. Mondays expect disaster. Fridays forgive it. Tuesday just wants to get through Tuesday.

It was yellow. Not metaphorically yellow — literally yellow, the color of caution tape and school buses and the kind of highlighter that makes you feel organized while you ignore your life. My mother put it on the kitchen table like it might explode. My sister circled it with her coffee mug. Nobody touched it for eleven minutes.

I touched it.

*NOTICE TO TERMINATE TENANCY.*

Seventeen years, boiled down to a font you use for parking tickets.

The owner — Mr. DeLuca, who used to mow the strip of grass between our driveway and his rental unit with a push mower older than I am, who once fixed our water heater in his Sunday clothes and refused a twenty — had died in March. Quietly. The way people die when they have been practicing being quiet their whole lives. His kids lived in Arizona. His kids had lawyers. His kids, apparently, had plans for the property that did not include us.

"We have rights," my sister said.

"We have boxes," I said.

"Wes."

"I'm just saying. Rights require paper. Boxes require tape."

My mother looked at both of us like we were appliances that had started making a new noise.

"Find the lease," she said.

So we went looking for a piece of paper that might save a life we were already half out of.

## 2. The House That Remembers

The house was in Chatsworth, which is Southern California's way of pretending the Valley has a foothills personality. Rock formations. Horse properties you can't afford to look at. A Target that feels like a town square. Seventeen years in one address is long enough for the drywall to learn your arguments.

We had lived there since I was twenty-three. I left once, came back twice, stayed through versions of myself that would not have shaken hands with each other. The house absorbed all of it without comment, which is what houses do until they don't.

Now every closet was a deposition.

We opened drawers like crime scenes. Filing cabinets. The junk drawer that had become a museum of rubber bands and expired warranties. The hall closet where Christmas lived in January. The garage, which we agreed to save for last the way you save the hardest conversation for after dinner and then never have dinner.

My sister found a folder labeled IMPORTANT.

Inside: a takeout menu from a Thai place that closed in 2011, three expired coupons, and a photograph of our dog who had been dead long enough that looking at him felt like looking at a younger brother.

"Important," I said.

"Don't."

"I'm not laughing at the dog."

"You're laughing near the dog."

She was right. I put the photograph on the KEEP pile and felt, for one clean second, like a decent person.

The lease was not in IMPORTANT.

The lease was not in TAXES 2008–2014.

The lease was not under the bed, in the piano bench, behind the water heater, or in the box marked MISC that had been MISC so long the word had lost its meaning and become a lifestyle.

California tenancy law swam around us in half-remembered articles from late-night phone scrolling. Just cause. Relocation. Rent control that did and did not apply depending on the building, the city, the year, the phase of the moon, and whether Mr. DeLuca had ever filed the right form with a county office that may or may not still exist under that name.

One missing document. One missing document and our future was a coin flip performed by strangers in Arizona.

I have had worse odds.
I have also had better ones.
The American specialty is teaching you both and then asking which one you are.

## 3. The Last Possible Moment

I waited until the last possible moment to start packing.

Not because I am lazy. Laziness gets a bad reputation. Laziness is often just grief with better branding.

I waited because packing is a confession.

You put a book in a box and you are saying: this room is over. You wrap a plate and you are saying: this table is temporary. You label a carton KITCHEN and you are saying the kitchen will be somewhere else, which means this kitchen — with the burn mark on the counter from the year I learned that pizza stones are not immortal — is already a memory wearing a present-tense disguise.

My mother packed like a general.
My sister packed like a prosecutor.
I packed like a man being asked to write his own obituary in Sharpie.

"You should start on the garage," my sister said.

"I will."

"You said that Monday."

"It was true Monday."

"It's Thursday."

"Then it's more true."

She left a roll of packing tape on my dresser like a threat.

That night I stood in the garage doorway with the light off and let the dark invent shapes out of seventeen years of accumulation. It smelled like oil and cardboard and the ghost of a lawnmower we sold in 2019. Somewhere in that dark was every version of me that had believed a purchase was a plan.

I turned on the light.

The garage looked back at me like a jury.

## 3B. How to Delay a Funeral for Your Life

There is an art to not starting.

You become very good at adjacent tasks. You clean the kitchen that is already clean. You call the DMV about a sticker that is not due. You reorganize the junk drawer into a more sophisticated junk drawer, with zones. You tell yourself this is preparation. Preparation is a flattering word for fear.

I made lists.

Lists are how modern people pray.

*Box sizes.*
*Hardware store.*
*Ask Monica about the truck.*
*Find lease.*
*Find lease.*
*Find lease.*

I wrote *find lease* three times as if repetition could shame a document into existing.

At night I walked the house like a security guard hired by nostalgia. Here is where I stood when I got the call that changed one year. Here is where I ate cereal over the sink during the broke months, developing a philosophy of standing meals. Here is the hallway light switch that still sticks, that I swore I would fix in 2012, 2015, 2019, and during one optimistic weekend in the pandemic when everyone briefly became a different person with tools.

I put my hand on the wall and thought: *you held us.*

The wall, being a wall, did not answer.
Walls understand their job is structural, not emotional. Humans keep getting this backward.

My sister found me like that and said, "If you're going to haunt the place, at least pack while you do it."

"I'm paying respects."

"Pay them in boxes."

## 3C. Seventeen Years in Bullet Points Nobody Asked For

I moved in at twenty-three with a duffel bag, a mattress, and the arrogance of a person who thought temporary meant short.

Then temporary grew roots.

Years happened the way years happen in the Valley: heat, Santa Ana winds, a season of rain that made everyone religious for nine days, another company restructuring, another birthday cake in the same kitchen under the same cheap chandelier that shed crystals like it was nervous.

I fell in love here once, badly, with intensity and a shared calendar.
I fell out of love here with less intensity and the same calendar, which is worse.
I got good news on the porch.
I got bad news in the hallway, which has better acoustics for bad news.
I learned to cook two excellent meals and twelve adequate ones.
I paid rent on time so often it became a personality trait, then a trap: the responsible one stays until responsibility becomes the only story available.

Seventeen years is long enough to become local without becoming landed.
You know the shortcuts. You know which cashier will let the card run twice. You know the sound of your own garage door and can identify it from the sidewalk like a birdcall.

And still the law can look at you and say: guest.

That word does something to the spine.

## 4. Inventory of a Man

Let me tell you what forty looks like from the inside of a garage in Chatsworth.

It looks like a mountain bike with two flat tires and a price tag still attached from the year I thought cardio would save my personality. It looks like a keyboard still in a box because I was waiting for the room where music would feel allowed. It looks like three half-built shelves, one completed shelf holding nothing, and a drill with a dead battery that somehow still made me feel capable when I held it.

It looks like the camping gear from the summer I was going to become outdoorsy.
It looks like the bread machine from the winter I was going to become wholesome.
It looks like the espresso machine from the spring I briefly had money and mistook caffeine for destiny.

America sells you futures in appliance form.
The garage is where those futures go to wait for a better version of you to pick them up.

I found a leather jacket I bought when I was twenty-eight and briefly dated a woman who liked rooftops. The jacket still fit. The rooftops did not. I put it in DONATE and then took it out and then put it back in DONATE and then hung it on a nail and called that a third category: PENDING.

PENDING is where most of a life goes when you are honest.

There was a box of cables for devices that no longer exist. I held a FireWire cable the way an archaeologist holds a bone. Somewhere, in a landfill or a drawer in Ohio, the machine that needed this cable was also wondering what happened to us.

Debt receipts in a folder marked, with devastating sincerity, FUTURE.
A bowling ball from a league I joined to meet people and then quit because I met people.
A trophy from a company softball team for a company that laid half of us off the following Monday. PARTICIPATION, the plaque said, which is American for *you showed up and capitalism noticed briefly.*

I sat on an overturned bucket and laughed until it turned into the other sound, and then I made it turn back into laughing, because I refuse to give this garage the satisfaction of a soundtrack.

## 4B. The Archaeology of Almost

The deeper I dug, the more I found the same man wearing different hobbies like seasonal coats.

There was the photography phase: two lenses, a tripod that always leaned south, memory cards full of sunsets that looked like everyone else's sunsets. I had believed that if I framed the world correctly, the world might return the favor.

There was the fermentation phase: crocks, airlocks, a cabbage-related incident we do not discuss in polite family court.

There was the brief, humiliating crypto window — printouts, passwords, a conviction that the future was a spreadsheet. The future, it turned out, was still rent.

I held up a yoga mat still coiled in plastic and saluted it like a fallen soldier.

"Namaste, never," I said.

From the doorway, my mother: "Who are you talking to?"

"My potential."

"Tell it we need the truck by Friday."

Potential does not do logistics. That is why people like me end up with garages.

I found a sealed box labeled WES ROOM — OLD APARTMENT. Inside were the artifacts of a man who had once lived alone and called it freedom: a rice cooker, a framed print of a Japanese wave, a corkscrew for wine I used to pretend I understood. Underneath, a letter I wrote and never sent to a woman named Rae. I read the first line — *I keep trying to become someone you'd recognize* — and folded it back up like a dangerous map.

Some papers are missing because the house ate them.
Some papers are missing because you hide them from yourself.
The lease might have been either.
The letter was definitely the second.

I put Rae's letter in a new envelope and wrote on the outside: DO NOT OPEN UNLESS YOU ARE BRAVER THAN PRESENT TENSE.

Then I put that envelope in DONATE by mistake, cursed, retrieved it, and filed it under PENDING with the jacket, the keyboard, and my entire personality.

## 5. Both Americas

People like to put you in one story.

Either you are the guy who had it easy, or the guy who got crushed.
Either you are privileged, or you are struggling.
Either you are the before picture or the after picture.

I have been both pictures in the same house.

There was a stretch — short, bright, stupid — when money arrived like weather. Contract work. A client with more budget than sense. I bought dinners that required a reservation and a personality. I tipped like I was auditioning for a better moral class. For six months I mistook comfort for character.

Then the work dried up the way California rivers dry up: gradually, then all at once, then with a news story about how nobody could have predicted the climate.

I learned how to stretch a paycheck until it became a philosophy.
I learned which bills could wait and which bills wait for no man.
I learned that poverty does not automatically make you wise any more than a gym membership automatically makes you athletic. It just makes you tired in a more educational way.

Luxury did not give me peace.
Struggle did not give me purity.
Both gave me stories I tell myself in parking lots.

What I wanted, sitting on that bucket under a fluorescent tube that buzzed like a warning, was not another peak or another lesson.

I wanted margin.

Not the rich kind.
The oxygen kind.

A life where a broken water heater is an inconvenience instead of a plot twist.
A life where creativity is not something you squeeze in between emergencies like a hobby you feel guilty for having.
A life where the home is a sanctuary instead of a countdown.

Margin.
The most American dream nobody puts on a bumper sticker.

## 5B. A Brief History of My Credit Score as a Weather System

If you want to know a man in America, do not ask about his dreams.
Ask about his autopays.

Mine have been a climate. There were tropical years, when money moved through the account with vacation energy. There were droughts. There was one winter where I paid the minimum on everything and called it strategy, the way you call a retreat a repositioning.

I am not ashamed of having been broke.
I am ashamed of how often I confused being flush with being fine.

When I had money, I bought solutions in boxes.
When I did not, I bought time in late fees.

Neither purchase delivered peace.
Peace, I was learning in a garage full of both eras, is not a product. It is a ratio: demands divided by capacity. Most of us are walking around with the numerator on fire.

My sister says I intellectualize pain.
I say I aestheticize accounting.
We are both right. That is why we survive each other.

## 5C. A Tour of the House for Nobody

Before we emptied it, I gave myself a private tour, whispering like a docent with no group.

*This is the living room where we watched a democracy argue with itself for a decade of evenings.*

*This is the hallway where everyone practiced apology faces before family meetings.*

*This is the bathroom mirror that told the truth only in the morning and lied politely at night.*

*This is the backyard where the lemon tree did its one job with more consistency than most institutions.*

*This is the garage. Please keep your hands inside the vehicle. Objects may reflect who you meant to become.*

I ended at the front door looking out, then looking in, then locking nothing because the danger was not intruders. The danger was time with a forwarding address.

If you have never walked a home you are about to lose, I will not explain the feeling. It is not sadness exactly. It is more like standing in a museum on the last day before the collection is broken up, trying to memorize the arrangement because the arrangement was the art.

## 6. Search Party

My mother found the original deed from a different property entirely and held it up with hope so bright I almost hated to correct her. My sister made a spreadsheet of places we had already looked, which is how control dresses for a funeral. I emptied a file box that contained, in this order:

1. My high school diploma
2. A timeshare brochure for Cabo
3. A handwritten note that said *call dentist*
4. A lease — wrong year, wrong address, different landlord, a ghost of a previous almost-life in Northridge

"Close," my sister said.

"Close is a word people use when they want credit for failing specifically."

She gave me the look she invented when we were kids and I still haven't found a legal defense against it.

Mr. DeLuca's son called from Scottsdale. He was polite the way people are polite when the outcome is already decided. He said he understood this was hard. He said they needed to settle the estate. He said if we found documentation of any special arrangement we should send it over.

Special arrangement.
As if seventeen years of living somewhere carefully were a handshake written in invisible ink.

"We'll find it," my mother told him.

After she hung up, she stood at the sink and washed a plate that was already clean.

That is the official dance of American crisis: perform usefulness until the feeling passes or the feeling graduates into a plan.

## 7. Dream: West of Everything

In the dream — and I am calling it a dream even when I am awake, because hope is just dreaming with better posture — I am driving.

Not escaping.
Arriving.

The freeway thins out. The billboards get honest. The mountains stop being a backdrop and start being geography. Somewhere past the last In-N-Out that feels obligatory, the light changes. California keeps going, but the expensive part of California gets bored and stays behind.

High desert.
Juniper.
A sky so wide it makes your problems look like they need to reapply for significance.

There is a small house that does not apologize for being small.
There is a workshop with a bench and light and room for unfinished things to remain unfinished without becoming moral failures.
There is a kitchen where cooking is not a race against the day.
There is music — not as content, not as background for productivity, just music, occupying air like it pays rent.

At night the stars are not a metaphor. They are a fact with no cover charge.

I stand outside with a coffee that tastes like coffee instead of a lifestyle, and for once my nervous system does not have a meeting scheduled with catastrophe.

In the dream I can hear myself think.

In Chatsworth, under the buzzing light, I could hear a neighbor's leaf blower and my own pulse and the cardboard screaming quietly as I taped another box shut.

## 7B. The Leaf Blower Industrial Complex

A word about suburban Southern California silence:

There is none.

There is always a leaf blower. Somewhere. If you cannot hear one, you are between leaf blowers, which is not the same as quiet. It is intermission.

The leaf blower is our civic instrument. It announces that nature has made a mess and a man in noise-canceling earmuffs has been hired to bully the mess into a different zip code. Leaves are not destroyed. Leaves are displaced. This is also how we handle housing, grief, and political conversations at Thanksgiving.

I stood in the garage listening to Owensmouth Avenue perform America: blower, distant freeway, a dog with opinions, a couple arguing in Spanish about a parking space with the passion of people who still believe parking is a moral issue.

In the dream town there is wind instead.
Wind does not care if you scheduled peace for Sunday.
Wind is honest.

I wrote on a piece of cardboard with a Sharpie:

WANTED: A LIFE WITH FEWER MACHINES APOLOGIZING FOR NATURE

And taped it above the workbench like a mission statement from a country that does not exist yet.

## 7C. Dad's Boats, Briefly

My father has been gone long enough that grief has become a roommate instead of a home invasion.

He collected magazines about boats. Not boats — magazines about boats. The distinction mattered to him not at all and to me increasingly. He liked the idea of open water from a recliner in a landlocked life. When he died, we kept the magazines because throwing them away felt like throwing away his ability to dream in a cheap, renewable form.

During the move I sat with a stack of them in the garage and looked at covers: white vessels, blue water, men with sunglasses that implied destinies.

"You never got a boat," I told the stack.

The stack did not argue.

I kept one issue — July 2004, a hull shining like a promise — and recycled the rest. My mother watched and nodded once, a tiny blessing. We are learning, late, that carrying someone's dream intact can become a way of not having your own.

Dad, if you are anywhere that still gets paper: I am heading inland, not toward a marina. Different map. Same hunger. I'll tell you about the shed when I get the radio working.

## 8. The Argument About the Couch

It was ugly in a committed way. Brown. Heavy. Stained with a history of snacks and naps and one New Year's Eve that taught us humility. It had held every version of this family through every version of television. My mother wanted to keep it. My sister wanted to burn it for the insurance money we did not have. I wanted to leave it on the curb with a sign that said FREE TO A GOOD HOME OR A CHAOTIC ONE I AM NOT PICKY.

"It still has life," my mother said.

"So did the lease," I said, "and look how that turned out."

We got loud. Then quiet. Then loud about the quiet. Classic family orchestration: everyone playing a different song in the same key of panic.

Finally I said, "If the new place has room, we keep it. If the new place is trying to become a life, we don't drag a museum into it."

My mother looked at me for a long time.

"You already decided we're leaving," she said.

"The notice decided."

"No," she said. "The notice threatened. You're the one leaving."

She was not wrong.
That was the worst part.
She was not wrong.

I went back to the garage and wrapped a lamp I hated like it was made of glass forgiveness.

## 9. Objects with Former Names

Every object in the garage had a former name.

The guitar case was *I'm going to learn fingerpicking this year.*
The snowboard was *Tahoe weekends with people who text back.*
The printer was *I'm going to be organized,* which is the funniest lie I ever bought for $179.

I found a box of notebooks.

Opening them was like reading letters from a hostage who sounded a lot like me. Half-finished songs. Business ideas with arrows and underlines. A page that just said, in large letters, START ANYWAY. Dated 2016. I had started anyway, repeatedly, and the evidence was scattered around me like a crime of optimism.

There was a suit from a job interview for a job I did not get at a company that no longer exists. The suit still looked like potential. That is the danger of suits. They keep advertising a future after the store has closed.

I tried on the leather jacket again in the side mirror of a car that wasn't there.

Forty looks like a man doing that and knowing exactly how dumb it is and doing it anyway because dignity is a group project and I was alone in a garage.

"You look like someone," my sister said from the doorway.

"Someone who?"

"Someone who used to think the jacket was the whole outfit."

I left it in PENDING.
PENDING had become its own zip code.

## 9B. Monica Brings Pizza and Unwanted Clarity

Monica from two doors down brought pizza because that is what good neighbors do when they smell cardboard and panic. She stood in our emptied dining room holding the box like an offering to a minor god of carbohydrates.

"So it's real," she said.

"It's real," my sister said.

"Where will you go?"

The question hung there with pepperoni. Where will you go is the suburban sequel to how are you. Nobody wants the true answer. The true answer is: into a thinner wallet and a wider sky and a version of myself I have not met without an audience of old furniture.

"Somewhere with less rent and more stars," I said.

Monica nodded as if I had said *Pasadena.*

"We looked for the lease," my mother added, because she still needed the sentence to exist in the world. "We almost had it."

Almost had it.
The family motto, embroidered on nothing.

After Monica left, we ate pizza on the floor and watched a show on a laptop about people buying houses with vaulted ceilings and the emotional range of real estate agents. My sister muted it and said, "We are the cautionary tale before the commercial break."

"Speak for yourself," I said. "I'm the redemption arc. I just haven't gotten to the music cue."

My mother took the last crust and said, quietly, "I liked it here."

Nobody joked after that for a while.
Even the leaf blower, for once, took a breath.

## 9C. What the Garage Knows About Love

I found a crate of photo albums.

Not the digital kind, where memory is a cloud owned by a company that will charge you later. The heavy kind. The kind that smell like attics and adhesive.

There we were: birthdays, barbecues, one Halloween where I went as a broken printer because I have always been too literal. Mr. DeLuca in the background of three photos, caught mid-wave, mid-laugh, mid-existence. Alive in the way photographs insist upon, which is rude to the present.

There was a picture of me at thirty-two on the roof with Christmas lights, triumphant and stupid, a man who believed tangled wires were a temporary condition.

I miss him.
Not because he was wiser.
Because he had not yet learned how many temporary conditions graduate into lifestyles.

Love lived in that house in ordinary forms: rides to the airport, soup when somebody was sick, the unspoken agreement that whoever got up first would start the coffee. Nobody writes novels about coffee duty. They should. Nations are built on smaller loyalties than that.

I closed the album and put it in KEEP without a hearing.
Some objects are not up for debate.
They are the minutes of the meeting where you decided to be a family.

## 10. American Migration, Local Edition

Here is the joke nobody laughs at hard enough:

I was born in America.
I have never left America for longer than a long weekend.
And still I am trying to get to America.

Not the brochure.
The version where ordinary peace is not a luxury brand.

My grandparents' people moved for work, for weather, for the rumor of a better floor plan for the soul. They crossed distances that made sense as myth. I was preparing to cross a distance measured in U-Haul days and Craigslist lies, chasing the same rumor in a Honda with balding tires.

The irony was so thick you could spread it on toast and call it heritage.

Coastal California had given me ocean light and avocado propaganda and a cost of living that treated breathing like a premium feature. I was not angry at the place. Anger requires a clean villain. California is too pretty to prosecute. It just quietly asks for more than you have until you start looking at maps like they are dating profiles.

High desert towns.
Mountain towns.
Places where a house might be a house instead of a audition.

I printed listings I could almost afford and taped them to the garage wall next to a faded poster of the solar system. Planets and floor plans. Same hunger: show me a place with room to orbit.

## 10B. Scottsdale Calls Again

Mr. DeLuca's son called back on speakerphone so the whole kitchen could share the experience of being managed from a desert with better branding.

His name was Paul. Paul had a voice like a golf shirt: soft, expensive, convinced the conversation was friendly.

"We're prepared to offer two weeks beyond the notice," Paul said. "As a courtesy."

Courtesy is when power holds the door one extra second and wants credit for chivalry.

"We still haven't found the original lease," my mother said.

"If it turns up, have your attorney send a copy."

*Your attorney.* As if we kept one in the pantry between the beans and the flashlights.

My sister took the phone. "Paul. Your father liked us. He said so. In handwriting."

"My father liked a lot of people," Paul said, not unkindly. "I'm tasked with the estate, not the scrapbook."

After the call I went outside and stood in the driveway looking at the house we were losing to paperwork gravity. The roof still needed the repair from the sticky note in 2014. The lemon tree still dropped fruit onto a sidewalk that did not care. The world is full of continuations that do not include you. That is not cruelty. That is physics with landscaping.

"Two weeks," my sister said, joining me.

"Courtesy."

"We take the courtesy. We use the courtesy. We do not write Paul a thank-you note."

"Agreed."

We stood there like people at the end of a dock watching a boat leave that we were never actually on.

## 11. What We Found Instead

We never found the lease.

We found a 2009 Christmas card from Mr. DeLuca that said *Happy Holidays to my favorite tenants* in handwriting so careful it looked like he had practiced on scrap paper first. We found a copy of a rent increase letter from 2014 with a sticky note in my mother's hand: *ask about roof leak.* We found proof we had lived carefully. We did not find proof the law was required to care.

On a Saturday, a lawyer friend of a friend told us, gently, that without the original agreement our leverage was a polite fiction. We could ask for time. We could ask for relocation money. We could not ask the past to produce a document it had apparently eaten.

My mother cried once, briefly, at the kitchen table, then wiped the table as if the tears were crumbs.

My sister closed her spreadsheet with a keystroke that sounded like a gavel.

I went to the garage and threw away the bread machine.

It felt like murdering a soft idea.
It also felt fantastic.

Freedom is disgusting when it first arrives. It smells like dust and plastic and the sudden absence of a future you were using as furniture.

## 11B. A Eulogy for Mr. DeLuca Delivered into a Shop Vac

I turned on the shop vac and spoke into its roar so nobody would accuse me of sentiment.

"You were a decent landlord," I told the dust. "Which is like being a decent shark. Statistically notable."

The vac screamed agreement.

"You fixed things. You knocked. You remembered my mother's birthday once and brought cannoli, which was confusing but welcome. You never explained your children, and now that I have met Paul over the phone I understand why. You occupied the strange American role of stranger-who-owns-your-ceiling. And you died, and the ceiling went to committee."

I vacuumed the same square of concrete three times.

"I hope wherever you are, somebody else is looking for a missing document and you are laughing gently. Or resting. Resting is fine. You earned the push mower silence."

When I shut the vac off, my ears rang with holy emptiness.
That was as close to church as the garage got.

## 11C. The Spreadsheet of the Soul

My sister emailed me a file named FINAL_SORT.xlsx.

Tabs:
- Furniture
- Kitchen
- Wes Special Problems
- Mother's Nonnegotiables
- Donate Routes
- Emotional Landmines (Do Not Joke)

I opened Emotional Landmines and found:

1. Lemon tree photos
2. Dad's magazines (boats)
3. The good ladle
4. Wes's notebooks — let him choose, do not hover
5. The couch — already litigated, appeals closed

I replied: *Tab 5 is the funniest thing you have ever written.*

She replied: *Be at Home Depot at 8. We need more tape and a rope that can handle your metaphors.*

This is love in a practical family: insult, logistics, loyalty, repeat.

## 11D. Work, or: How I Afforded the Illusion of Stability

I should say what I do, before the book pretends money is only a weather system.

I do a handful of things that add up to a living if nobody gets sick and the car does not invent a new noise. Graphic work for small businesses. Occasional music for people who still pay for music. A warehouse stretch when the freelance apps went quiet and pride had to wait in the break room with everybody else's pride. I am not unemployable. I am diversified in the way dandelions are diversified.

There were years I made enough to feel adult in restaurants.
There were years I made enough to feel adult in a different way: calling the dentist and asking about payment plans with the calm of a hostage negotiator.

The house in Chatsworth made both versions look the same from the street. That is suburbia's magic trick. Everyone's lawn can imply a novel that is not the novel.

When the notice came, I calculated runway on a napkin.
The napkin lost.

So the move was not only spiritual. It was arithmetic with a sunrise on top.

I need you to hold both truths at once:
I was choosing a life with margin.
I was also fleeing a zip code that had begun charging extra for oxygen.

If that makes me less pure, good. Purity is expensive. I am shopping in a different aisle now.

## 11E. Saturday: The Long Haul Inside One Address

Saturday began with coffee and the kind of sunrise that makes Southern California look innocent.

We worked like people paid by the hour by Death.

Morning: kitchen. Bubble wrap. The good ladle wrapped like a relic. My mother crying once into a dish towel and pretending it was dust. My sister labeling with a label maker she bought for this crisis and will keep for the next one.

Midday: hallway closet. Board games with missing pieces. A vacuum cleaner that still worked and therefore felt like a moral victory. Coats from colder ambitions.

Afternoon: my room. The hardest geography. Clothes that belonged to different waistlines of the self. Books I had used as personality and books I had actually read — two overlapping but distinct religions. I donated the ones I kept to impress women who now have children named after hikers.

Evening: garage again. Always garage again. The black hole at the center of the moving universe.

I carried out the mountain bike. A man in a Toyota offered me forty dollars before it hit the curb.

"It has flat tires," I said.

"So do my dreams," he said, and we completed the transaction like Americans who recognize each other across a brief marketplace of defeat.

By night the driveway looked like a yard sale hosted by ghosts.
We sat on the KEEP couch in the middle of it all and ate burritos and watched the sky go theatrical behind the rock formations.

"I keep waiting to feel tragic," I said.

"Do you?" my sister asked.

"I feel busy. And occasionally holy. And then hungry again."

"That's not tragedy," my mother said. "That's a move."

## 12. Reality: Trash Bags

Reality is trash bags that rip on the one sharp thing you forgot.
Reality is bubble wrap screaming under your hands.
Reality is deciding whether a cracked Pyrex dish is thrift-store charming or cursed.
Reality is your back announcing, at thirty-nine becoming forty, that it has opinions.

We filled the driveway with piles:

KEEP.
DONATE.
TRASH.
MYSTERIOUSLY EMOTIONAL.

The neighbors walked past with the stiff faces of people who have smelled an ending and do not want it on their shoes. One of them offered us boxes. One of them offered thoughts and prayers, which in suburban Southern California means a wave from a Tesla.

I carried a dresser to the curb and felt my timeline rearrange. Somewhere in my twenties I had assembled this dresser with a friend who now lived in Portland and posted about trail runs. The dresser had outlasted the friendship's active hours. Furniture is loyal in a way people can't afford to be.

"Last pile," my sister said, nodding at the garage.

"It's not a pile. It's an archive."

"Archives burn, Wes. Or they move. Pick one."

---

## 12B. The Donation Center Prophet

The donation center was a warehouse of soft landings.

We drove up with a Honda full of eras. A man with a clipboard and a face like weathered kindness waved us toward a bay. Behind him, mountains of couches, toys, clothes — America's surplus soul, sorted by day of the week.

"Any mattresses?" he asked.

"Only emotional ones," I said.

He did not laugh. He had heard every joke a move can manufacture.

We unloaded the espresso machine, the yoga mat, the suit, boxes of kitchenware that had survived marriages they were not invited to. Each object passed from my hand to a stranger's and became, instantly, not mine. The relief was physical. The grief was also physical. They shared a parking space in my chest.

"You're doing the right thing," the clipboard man said, automatic.

"I don't know what I'm doing."

He looked at me then, actually. "Nobody does. They just arrive with trucks."

On the way out I saw a kid leave with a bike that looked like my mountain bike's cousin. He was already smiling into a future where the tires still held air. Good. Let someone else ride the almost. I was done collecting almosts like they were going to mature into savings bonds.

---

## 12C. Night Drive to Nowhere in Particular

I could not sleep, so I drove.

Not toward the new town — not yet. Just the old freeways, the ones that taught me geography as a circulatory system. The 118. The 405 looking mean even at midnight. Topanga Canyon for ten minutes until the curves reminded me I was exhausted and mortal.

I parked at a overlook and watched the Valley's lights perform their endless audition.

Somewhere down there: the house, half-packed, humming with the anxiety of women I love. Somewhere out past the dark edge: the other life, not yet real enough to hurt me with disappointment.

I thought about agency again. The word had become a pebble in my shoe I refused to remove because it reminded me I was walking.

What did I want, stripped of poetry?

A rent I could pay without theater.
A room where I could make things without apologizing to the calendar.
A nervous system that was not always bracing for a yellow envelope.

Was that too much to ask from the richest myth on earth?

The Valley lights did not answer.
They just kept advertising permanence to people in temporary arrangements.

I drove home at 1:00 a.m. and packed three more boxes in a trance of progress. When I finally slept, I dreamed of a workshop, and this time there was no packing tape in my hand when I woke — only the ghost of a hammer and a feeling like a door cracking open.

## 13. Dream: The Workshop

In the dream the workshop is already mine.

Not fancy.
Functional the way a good sentence is functional.

A bench made of something honest. Pegboard with outlines waiting for tools. A radio that only knows one station and that station is enough. Projects mid-breath: a repaired chair, a half-sanded shelf, a song written on the wall in pencil because paper makes ideas get formal too fast.

I cook dinner after. Something with garlic. Something that makes the small house smell like a decision. Music on in the kitchen — old stuff, new stuff, the playlist of a man who has stopped performing his taste for an imaginary audience.

Outside: dark.
Above: stars with no cover charge.
Inside: the rare American silence that is not loneliness.

Margin has a sound.
It sounds like nothing chasing you.

Then a car alarm goes off on Owensmouth and I am back in Chatsworth with packing tape stuck to my own sleeve like a reminder that dreams do not load the truck for you.

---

## 14. The Night I Almost Kept Everything

There was a night — 1:14 a.m., moths at the garage light, the house behind me asleep and angry about it — when I decided to keep everything.

Not possible.
Still decided.

I walked the perimeter of the garage touching objects like a priest with no religion worth the name. Every abandoned hobby was a breadcrumb back to a man who still believed effort had a customer service department. Every cheap tool was evidence I had tried to be handy. Every expensive tool was evidence I had tried to be the kind of man who owns expensive tools.

If I threw it all away, who would testify that I had attempted a life?

That is the trap of stuff.
It pretends to be proof.

But proof of what? That I purchased? That I planned? That I got tired?

I sat down on the cold concrete and told the truth to a closed garage door:

I am not losing everything.
I am being asked to choose what comes with me.

And some of what I called *my life* was just *my inventory.*

The difference matters.
One of them can love you back.

---

## 14B. Dream: Kitchen at 6:42 p.m.

In the dream the kitchen windows face west.

I know it is 6:42 because the light has that particular gold that makes even cheap counters look like a decision God ratified. I am cutting onions. I am not crying about the onions or about anything else, which in the dream feels like a special effect.

Music on — something with brushes on a snare, something patient.
A glass of water, not wine. I am not performing sophistication for the cabinets.
Outside, heat lifts off the dirt road and the first star clocks in early like an eager employee.

Someone laughs in the next room. Could be my mother. Could be my sister. Could be a future person I have not met because I am still too busy surviving to be interesting. The laugh is enough. I do not need to brand it.

I plate the food.
I sit.
I eat without scrolling.

This last detail is how I know it is a dream.
In Chatsworth I once ate a burrito with one hand and opened three PDFs with the other and called that dinner.

When I blink, the onion in my real hand is a roll of packing tape, and my eyes are dry, and the garage light is the color of bureaucracy.

---

## 14C. Sorting Through Everything I Have Ever Been

By the third week the garage stopped being storage and became autobiography with dust.

I made piles not only by KEEP / DONATE / TRASH but by era, just to see the shape of the animal.

*The Trying-to-Be-Impressive Years:* the suit, the jacket, the espresso machine, a business-card holder still holding business cards for a version of me who believed networking was a personality.

*The Trying-to-Be-Holy Years:* unread books about habit formation, a meditation cushion with the tag on, a journal with three pages filled and ninety-seven pages of lined accusation.

*The Trying-to-Be-Fine Years:* duplicate chargers, canned beans, a first-aid kit used mostly for hangnails and existential dread, receipts folded into origami of denial.

Looking at it laid out, I felt less like a failure and more like a crowded apartment building where every tenant was me and none of them talked to each other.

"You had eras," my sister said, leaning on a broom.

"I had phases."

"Phases are eras that didn't get funding."

She was not wrong.
She is rarely wrong. It is her worst quality and her best.

I took one notebook from the holy years, one from the impressive years, and one blank one for whoever I was becoming. The rest went into recycling with a tenderness I usually reserve for living things.

You cannot take every draft into the next edition.
That is not betrayal.
That is editing.

## 15. Family Meeting, Informal, Hostile, Necessary

We sat on the floor because the table was in a box labeled TABLE, which felt like conceptual art.

"I found a place," I said.

My mother looked up too fast. My sister looked up too slow. Both meant the same thing: tell us the damage.

It was not coastal. It was not impressive. It was a two-bedroom on the edge of a high desert town where the wind means it and the rent did not require a blood oath. Small yard. A shed that could become a workshop if I stopped calling my fantasies fake before they grew legs. Forty minutes from a Costco, which is how you measure civilization after a certain age.

"When would we go?" my mother asked.

"We," I said carefully, "can mean a lot of things."

Silence. The good kind. The kind that has work to do.

My sister said, "You're taking the jacket."

"The jacket is PENDING."

"Take the jacket. Leave the bread machine's memory. Start there."

My mother reached over and squeezed my wrist once, hard, like she was checking for a pulse in my future.

"Your father would have kept the couch," she said.

"Dad also kept every issue of a magazine about boats he never bought."

"Don't speak ill of the boats."

We laughed. It was small and real and did not fix the notice on the fridge. It didn't need to. Not everything kind has to be useful.

## 15B. The Version of America Where Peace Is Attainable

I am aware of the irony. I am practically married to it.

A man born into the empire, searching within the empire for a county where the empire charges less for quiet. Migration without a border crossing. Exile with a forwarding address. The covered wagons in my bloodline would find my U-Haul spiritually familiar and technologically disappointing.

Still — the dream remains so old it loops back into dignity:

Go west until the cost of being yourself drops below the cost of performing survival.

For my people the west was once California.
For me California became the east of something further — elevation, dryness, a town with fewer influencers per capita, a night sky that does not require an app subscription to notice.

I am not saying the high desert is paradise.
Paradise is a marketing department.
I am saying paradise was never the assignment.

The assignment is a kitchen, a shed, a rent that leaves oxygen in the month, and enough unclaimed afternoon to hear a thought all the way to its end.

If that is childish, fine. Most adult goals are just childhood goals wearing khakis.

---

## 15C. Signing Day

We signed the new papers at a coffee shop near the hardware store in the destination town, one week before the Chatsworth keys surrendered.

The landlord was a woman named Deb who wore jeans like a fact and said, "I don't do drama. I do receipts." I loved her immediately in a non-romantic, highly practical way.

My mother asked about the water heater.
My sister asked about trash day.
I asked about the shed: "Can I put a bench in there? Electricity?"

Deb shrugged. "It's a shed, not a church. Improve it. Just don't burn down my property and we'll get along."

We signed.
The pen felt heavier than Paul's courtesy.

Outside, I shook Deb's hand and felt the first true click of the new life: not hope, not fear — *contract.* A chosen constraint. Agency is not the absence of paperwork. Agency is being the one who picks which paperwork can eat your years.

Driving back to Chatsworth that evening, the sunset was so aggressively beautiful I laughed out loud in the car alone.

"All right," I told the sky. "You don't have to oversell it."

The sky, like the wall in the old house, did not answer.
It just kept doing its job, which is more than I can say for certain estates in Arizona.

## 16. Loading

Moving day was a slapstick tragedy performed by amateurs.

The U-Haul smelled like every other family's crisis. The ramp had opinions. I lost a staring contest with a mattress and reinjured an old sports injury from a sport I never played. My sister directed traffic with the energy of a woman who would have done well in a war if wars were about lamps.

A box labeled FRAGILE turned out to contain sweatshirts.
A box labeled MISC turned out to contain the will to continue.
I dropped a toolbox and every tool escaped like hamsters.

"Smooth," my sister said.

"I'm building character."

"Build it faster."

Mr. DeLuca's son had sent a cleaner to walk through after us. The cleaner arrived early and stood on the sidewalk looking at our life like it was a before picture. I almost apologized to him. Then I didn't. Americans apologize too much for taking up space they paid for.

When the truck was full, the house looked stunned.

Empty rooms are louder than full ones. They make you hear the years.

I stood in the garage last. One nail in the wall. A oil stain like a continent. The solar system poster ghosted in a rectangle of less-dirty paint. Seventeen years, reducible to a cleaner’s checklist and a lighter Honda.

I hit the garage door button.
It closed like an eyelid.

## 16B. Final Walkthrough

The cleaner waited outside. Paul had hired him; Paul was thorough about erasing.

I walked the empty house alone.

My footsteps sounded like a different species of claim. In the bedroom: a lost sock under the radiator, brave as a final settler. In the bathroom: hard-water ghost of a thousand showers. In the kitchen: the burn mark on the counter. I pressed my thumb to it and left no new print worth documenting.

"Thank you," I told the house.

Saying thank you to architecture is embarrassing until you consider the alternatives: saying nothing, or pretending you were only ever a customer.

On the fridge, where the yellow notice had lived, there was a clean rectangle of brighter white. Absence with a tan line. I almost photographed it. Then I didn't. Not everything needs to become content. Some things can just finish.

Outside, my sister honked once — soft, human, *come on.*

I locked the door and put the key under the mat for the cleaner, as instructed, and felt the absurdity of carefully securing a place that had already decided to become someone else's investment.

## 16C. Coming to America

It started in my head before the radio had a chance.

Not the song itself — I am not made of money, and the universe does not sync-license your breakdowns — but the *feeling* of it. That Neil Diamond swagger of arrival. Brass and promise. A chorus built like a dock for tired ships. *Coming to America.* The whole myth in a title: you are heading toward the place where the story gets better.

Except I was already here.

Born here.
Raised here.
Seventeen years of rent paid into the coastal postcard.

I was not coming to America.
I was leaving the expensive exhibit of it.

Still, the feeling would not get out of the truck. It rode shotgun with the boxes. It hummed under the U-Haul's complaints. It made the whole departure feel like a parade held for an immigrant who had somehow immigrated sideways — out of the zip code everyone else was still trying to enter.

---

Southern California slid past the mirrors like a brochure that had learned to drive.

Stucco developments repeating themselves until the eye gave up counting. Beige, then a braver beige, then a gated beige with a fountain that suggested water was a lifestyle. Houses priced like apologies from God. Lawns so disciplined they looked afraid. In one driveway, a man washed a car that already looked washed. In another, nobody was outside at all — beautiful rooms facing beautiful streets, curtains drawn against the pressure of belonging.

Traffic braided and unbraided. Brake lights like a nervous system. Everyone going somewhere important enough to sit still for forty minutes and call it movement. Billboards sold teeth, lawsuits, cannabis, and gratitude journals. The American dream, itemized and financed.

I watched a couple argue silently in a Lexus at a red light — mouths moving, windows up, wealth insulating even their conflict. Beside them, a bus exhaled people who looked like they had already spent the day surviving the cost of being near the ocean.

This was the location of the dream.
You could take a picture of it and raise money.
You could die of loneliness in it with excellent weather.

The feeling in my head kept swelling anyway — patriotic, ridiculous, sincere despite itself — as if the country were throwing me a welcome party for exiting the part of itself that charged admission to breathe.

---

Boxes shifted behind me with every lane change. A lamp tapped its shade against a milk crate in 4/4 time. My mother's car followed with the plants leaning like passengers who disapproved of wind. My sister's brake lights winked whenever she thought I was driving like a metaphor.

The 118 gave way to the 14. The city began to lose interest in us.

First the density thinned.
Then the billboards got lonelier.
Then the mountains stopped being a backdrop and started being geology — real, indifferent, older than anybody's listing price.

Los Angeles fell away in the mirrors the way a loud conversation falls away when you step outside. Still there. No longer in charge of your pulse.

Desert opened its hands.

Scrub. Power lines. A sky with room in it. Light that did not care about your resume. The highway stretched east like a sentence finally allowed to run on.

And the song-feeling — God help me — got bigger. Not ironic enough to die, not sincere enough to be clean. Both at once. A satire that saluted. A salute that knew it was funny.

*Coming to America,* said the myth in my skull.
*You're already American,* said my passport.
*Then why,* said the boxes, *does this feel like arrival?*

Because the dream was never only the map.

People cross oceans for the feeling: a modest home, work that does not eat the whole animal of you, a kitchen, a little land if you are lucky, quiet enough to hear your own mind form a complete thought, enough margin that an emergency is not the only plot available.

I had lived inside the dream's famous coordinates and missed its sensation.
So I was migrating toward the sensation with a truck full of edited years, leaving the coordinates to Paul, to the cleaner, to the next tenants who would learn the sticky light switch and believe, for a while, that temporary meant short.

---

A flag snapped over a roadside equipment yard — ordinary, faded, doing its job without a speech.

I felt something stupid and real rise in my throat. Not nationalism. Not the commercial break kind of pride. Something more embarrassing: gratitude to a country spacious enough to fail you in one zip code and still have another zip code left to try. Satire included. Hypocrisy included. Leaf blowers included. The whole cracked cathedral of it.

"What are you smiling at?" my sister's text asked, because she could see my face in her mind from two cars back, the way siblings do.

I voice-to-text at a red light in a town that was mostly exit ramp:

*neil diamond is bullying me*

She sent back a flag emoji and a middle finger emoji, which is the correct bilingual response to America.

Green light.
Highway again.
Mountains arranging themselves into a future I was still not allowed to romanticize.

I romanticized them anyway —
an immigrant to my own nation,
coming to America from America,
looking for the version where the promise is not a view you can't afford to feel.

---

## 17. Highway

There is a moment on the 14 where Los Angeles loosens its grip and pretends it never loved you.

The truck shook. The radio finally caught up to my head and found a classic rock station that believed in guitar solos as a moral good — not the Diamond song, which would have been too on the nose even for fate. My mother followed in her car with the plants. My sister posted nothing, which was how I knew she was present.

The welcome-parade feeling faded into ordinary road noise, which was a relief. Myths are exhausting when they ride the whole way.

I watched the mountains continue their work.

I thought about the missing lease — that sacred missing American document, the one piece of paper that was supposed to prove we deserved to stay. We never found it. Maybe it never existed in the form we needed. Maybe it had been real and the house ate it. Maybe the lesson was insultingly simple: your life cannot be saved by paperwork you cannot produce, only by motion you are willing to make.

Darkly funny.
Brutally true.
Also annoying.
Growth is annoying. That is how you know it isn't marketing.

At a gas station in the middle of nowhere-adjacent, I bought coffee that tasted like a burnt offering and stood watching a kid chase a plastic bag across the lot like it was a pet with ambition. His mother laughed into her phone. A trucker stared at the mountains with the expression of a man who has already forgiven them for being beautiful.

This is the America under the America: transient, fluorescent, oddly gentle between transactions.

"You okay?" my sister asked, appearing with trail mix.

"I'm between selves."

"Eat something. Between selves still needs protein."

We clinked coffee cups like we were toasting a wedding of necessity and hope. Then we got back on the road because the U-Haul charged by the day and capitalism does not pause for metaphor, even when metaphor is the whole trip.

## 17B. First Sight of the Town

The town did not roll out a parade.

It offered a main street with a hardware store that looked open on principle, a coffee shop named after a mineral, a vacant lot where a future argument about parking would someday occur. Beyond that: houses with solar panels and houses with trucks and houses with both, which is the new American flag if we were honest.

Our turn came up on the GPS with the smug calm of a machine that has never lost a lease.

"This is it?" my mother said, not disappointed — calibrating.

"This is it," I said.

The hill in the window was real. The shed was real. The air smelled like dust and sage and a little like freedom if freedom has a scent, which it does: it smells like a place that has not already spent your paycheck in advance.

My sister exhaled. "Okay."

One word.
The official acceptance stamp of our bloodline.

---

## 18. The New Place

The new place was smaller than memory and bigger than fear.

Beige carpet with ambitions of gray. A kitchen with exactly enough counter space to cook or argue, not both. A window that framed a hill like it was doing us a favor. The shed out back was crooked in a charming way if you were already committed to loving it.

We unloaded until our arms filed complaints with the union of the body.

Then — because nobody had energy for grandeur — I did something small.

I plugged in a cheap speaker.
I put on a record — okay, a playlist pretending to be a record; we live in the year we live in — and let music walk into the rooms before the furniture finished deciding where it belonged.
I boiled pasta.
I made sauce from a jar I improved like a man still trying to have standards without a trust fund.
I set three plates on a table that wobbled until my sister shoved a folded moving receipt under one leg, which was the most honest furniture repair I have ever seen.

We ate.

No speeches.
No new-leaf ceremony.
Just garlic and carbohydrates and the strange peace of being too tired to catastrophize at full volume.

Outside, the desert evening came on fast, like a curtain with a union job.
Stars punched through.
No cover charge.

---

## 18B. First Morning

I woke at 5:40 without an alarm, which either meant peace or anxiety wearing peace's pajamas.

The house made unfamiliar clicks. New houses — new to you — spend a while translating themselves. I lay in the dark and inventoried what did not hurt: neck, okay; back, negotiable; finances, quiet until business hours; heart, surprisingly not performing opera.

I made coffee in a machine that had survived the move, which made it family.

Outside, the cold had edges. Chatsworth cold is polite. High desert cold tells the truth. I walked to the shed in socks, regretted the socks, continued anyway because character is partly poor decision-making you commit to.

I stood in the shed doorway and measured it with my eyes. Bench along that wall. Pegboard there. A shelf for paint. A corner for the keyboard if the keyboard ever leaves the box, and if it doesn't, a corner for the box, which is still more honest than a garage that swallowed ambitions whole.

"I can work with this," I said.

A bird answered in a language I did not need translated.

When I came back inside, my mother was awake at the table with her hands around a mug.

"Your father would've hated this carpet," she said.

"Dad hated most carpets. It was a belief system."

She smiled. "Do we have eggs?"

"We have eggs."

"Then we have a morning."

That is the whole recipe.

## 18C. The Call I Didn't Answer

Paul called once more while I was in the hardware store buying brackets and a level.

I watched the phone buzz in my pocket next to a bin of screws and felt no urge to extend the story. Some plots end mid-sentence and the sentence is still grammatical.

I bought the brackets. I bought the level. I bought a broom that would live in the shed and become, through use, a sacred object.

On the sidewalk outside, two teenagers argued about a band I had never heard of. Their urgency was beautiful. I hope they never find a missing lease. I hope their crises stay stupid and bright for as long as possible.

My phone buzzed again — a text from my sister:

*Shed dimensions? I'm making a plan.*

Of course she was.

I texted back measurements and a heart made of punctuation, then drove home through a late afternoon that looked like it had been color-corrected by someone who still believed in the West.

---

## 19. What a Home Is

I used to think a home was what you managed to keep.

The address you defended.
The rooms that knew your stubbornness.
The lease — preferably findable — that said yes, you belong here, in writing, with signatures, with the county's bored approval.

Now I think a home is also what you are willing to make after the old yes expires.

Not a perfect life.
God no. The sink already drips. The wind talks too much. I still have debt with the personality of a recurring villain. My mother misses the lemon tree. My sister has already found something wrong with the closet shelving and I love her for the consistency.

But tonight there is margin.

Not a lot.
Enough.

Enough that while the sauce simmered I stood at the kitchen window and heard myself think one clear thought without an emergency talking over it:

*You can build from here.*

Not a castle.
A bench in a shed.
A meal.
A morning.
A life with enough room to turn around in without knocking over your own panic.

I put the leather jacket on a hook by the door.
Not PENDING.
Just here.

I washed the plates. The warm water felt like a small country with open borders. Through the window I could see the shed, crooked, waiting for a bench and a radio and unfinished projects that would no longer mean I was failing — only that I was in process, which is the only honest state of a creative life.

My mother hummed something old while wiping the counter.
My sister emailed herself a new spreadsheet titled *NOT A CRISIS — A SETUP.*

Outside, somewhere past the first dark hill, America kept doing its loud circus of winners and debts and miraculous parking-lot sunsets. Inside, the music played, the cheap table held, and for the first time in a long time I was not surviving a storm.

I was arranging a room after one.

Which is not everything.

But it is not nothing.

And nothing was what the yellow notice had wanted me to become.

## 19B. Agency, Not Rescue

I need to be clear about the ending, because stories love to lie here.

Nobody saved me.
No sudden inheritance, no perfect job, no cinematic apology from Paul in Scottsdale. The missing lease stayed missing, a perfect little American ghost. We still had money fear. We still had old injuries. My mother still woke at three some nights and walked a house that did not yet know her footsteps.

What changed was the ratio.

Fewer emergencies per square foot.
More morning before the day starts collecting tolls.
A door I could close and hear only the wind, not the leaf blower industrial complex performing suburban war.

I do not want to become rich.
Rich is a moving target owned by people who are already bored.

I want agency.
The ability to choose the next small thing without the previous large thing choking the air out of the room.

Tonight I had that, briefly, like a match flame.
Tomorrow it might flicker.
I will still have matches.

---

## 19C. Music On, Sauce Simmering

I want to leave you with the sensory facts, because meaning gets slippery and garlic does not.

The speaker on the counter vibrates a little on the bass notes.
The window is cracked and the desert air comes in smelling like cooled dust and someone's distant fireplace.
My sister is on the floor assembling a shelf with the level I bought, cursing affectionately at an Allen wrench.
My mother is in the other room hanging the one good painting we kept — a landscape that is not California and therefore feels like permission.
I stir the sauce. Taste. Add salt. Taste again. This is the opposite of a yellow notice. This is a green light so ordinary it could be missed by anyone waiting for fireworks.

I think about the garage in Chatsworth, closed now, its continent of oil still on the floor for the next life to interpret. I think about the missing lease, still missing, perfect in its absence, a teacher that never showed up and still changed the curriculum. I think about Paul in Scottsdale and hope he finds, someday, a problem that cannot be solved by courtesy weeks.

Mostly I think: *I can hear myself.*

Not as a brand.
Not as a crisis manager.
As a person in a kitchen in a town that does not know my name yet, which is a gift disguised as anonymity.

The timer dings.
Pasta done.
Life, for the length of a meal, manageable.

That is the small victory.
I will not inflate it for the ending.
I will just live it, which is harder and better.

## 20. Everything Must Go

Later, alone, I wrote a list on the back of the moving receipt that leveled the table:

Things that left:
- the lease we never found
- the bread machine
- the idea that staying is the only form of winning
- seventeen years of dust shaped like my outline

Things that stayed:
- the jacket
- the notebooks (two; the rest forgiven)
- my sister's terrifying competence
- my mother's clean-plate grief
- the hunger for margin
- the hands that can still build a shelf if the shelf doesn't need to be perfect

Things I am taking that you cannot pack:
- the knowledge that I have lived many lives inside one life
- the refusal to be a victim in a story that still has episodes left
- a stubborn, ridiculous faith that peace can be affordable somewhere west of the worst rent

I turned off the kitchen light.
Left the hallway light on.
Stood in the new dark listening to the house settle, the way houses do when they are deciding whether to trust you.

"All right," I said to no one, to everyone, to Mr. DeLuca, to the missing paper, to the garage in Chatsworth closing its eyelid forever.

"All right. Let's make something here."

Outside, a dog barked once and thought better of it.
The stars held their positions.
The pasta pot soaked in the sink.

A small victory.
The kind you don't post.
The kind you live inside until it becomes ordinary —
which is, in the end, the whole point of peace.
